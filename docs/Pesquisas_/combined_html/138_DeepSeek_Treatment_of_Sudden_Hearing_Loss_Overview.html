1.2.3 Clinical Definition and Urgency

Sudden Sensorineural Hearing Loss (SSNHL) is most commonly defined as a sensorineural hearing loss of ‚â•30 dB affecting at least three consecutive frequencies that occurs over a 72-hour period or less. It is a medical emergency.

The most critical step is immediate medical evaluation (ideally within 24-48 hours) to rule out treatable causes like autoimmune disease, acoustic neuroma, or Meniere‚Äôs disease, and to initiate treatment promptly. Delayed treatment is strongly associated with poorer outcomes.

1.2.4 First-Line Treatment: Corticosteroids

The cornerstone of treatment for idiopathic SSNL (where no specific cause is found, which is about 90% of cases) is corticosteroid therapy, which reduces inflammation and swelling in the inner ear.

1.2.4.1 1. Systemic Corticosteroids

  • Protocol: Oral Prednisone, typically starting at a dose of 1 mg/kg/day (often a 60 mg daily dose) for 7-14 days, followed by a taper over a similar period.
  • Evidence: This has been the standard of care for decades, largely based on clinical experience and historical practice. A landmark study (the ‚ÄúWeiss‚Äù study) in 1980, while methodologically flawed by modern standards, solidified its use.
  • Time Sensitivity: Treatment is most effective when started within 72 hours of onset, but can still be beneficial up to 2-4 weeks.

1.2.4.2 2. Intratympanic (IT) Corticosteroids

This involves injecting steroids directly through the eardrum into the middle ear, where they are absorbed into the inner ear. This is now a first-line option alongside or instead of oral steroids.

  • Advantages:
    • Delivers a much higher concentration of the drug to the target site (the cochlea).
    • Avoids the systemic side effects of oral steroids (e.g., hyperglycemia, insomnia, mood swings).
    • The preferred option for patients with contraindications to systemic steroids (e.g., uncontrolled diabetes).
  • Protocol: Typically, a series of 3-4 injections over several weeks.
  • Evidence: Strong evidence supports its efficacy, both as a salvage therapy and as initial treatment.

Current State of Science on Steroid Regimens: The debate is no longer if to use steroids, but how best to use them. The current standard, supported by clinical guidelines, is often combination therapy (simultaneous oral and IT steroids) or sequential therapy (IT steroids as salvage if oral steroids fail).

1.2.5 Adjuvant and Investigational Treatments

For patients who do not respond adequately to steroids, or in specific cases, other treatments are considered.

1.2.5.1 Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy (HBOT)

  • Mechanism: Increases oxygen delivery to the hypoxic inner ear, reducing edema and promoting healing.
  • Evidence: The evidence is mixed but generally positive. Meta-analyses suggest that HBOT combined with steroid therapy improves recovery rates compared to steroids alone, especially when initiated early.
  • Current Status: Considered a Level 2 (option) in guidelines, often used as salvage therapy or in severe cases. Access can be limited.

1.2.5.2 Antiviral Therapy

  • Mechanism: Based on the theory that a viral infection (e.g., herpes simplex) may trigger SSNHL.
  • Evidence: Large-scale studies and meta-analyses have consistently shown that adding antivirals (like acyclovir or valacyclovir) to corticosteroids provides no additional benefit over steroids alone. Consequently, routine use of antivirals is no longer recommended.

1.2.5.3 Other Therapies (Lower Evidence)

  • Vasoactive Substances/Vasodilators: (e.g., pentoxifylline, prostaglandins). The theory of improving cochlear blood flow is intuitive, but robust clinical evidence is lacking. Not routinely recommended.
  • Antioxidants: (e.g., N-acetylcysteine, Ginkgo biloba). The role of oxidative stress in hearing loss is being investigated, but evidence for clinical efficacy is insufficient.
  • Magnesium: Some small studies suggest a potential benefit, but it is not a standard treatment.

1.2.6 Summary of the Current Standard of Care (Based on Clinical Guidelines)

  1. Immediate Referral: Patient presents with SOHL -> immediate referral to ENT.
  2. Workup: MRI with contrast to rule out vestibular schwannoma; blood tests to rule out autoimmune or other systemic causes.
  3. Initial Treatment (if idiopathic): Start corticosteroids as soon as possible, ideally within 72 hours.
    • Option A: Systemic (oral) steroids.
    • Option B: Intratympanic steroids (especially for contraindications to systemic steroids).
    • Option C (Growing in popularity): Combined systemic and intratympanic steroids.
  4. Salvage Therapy: For patients with incomplete recovery after initial oral steroid treatment, a course of intratympanic steroid injections is recommended.
  5. Adjuvant Therapy: Hyperbaric Oxygen Therapy can be considered, particularly for severe or recalcitrant cases.
